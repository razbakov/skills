# Enablers of Co-Creation

These patterns create the conditions for effective collaboration. Some apply to the organization's culture, others to its financial and legal structure.

## Financial Patterns

### Financial Transparency

Share relevant financial information with organization members to build trust and support informed decisions:

- Make financial performance data accessible to all members
- Include revenue, costs, margins, and resource allocation
- Members can make better decisions when they understand the financial context

**For AI agent organizations:** When agents make scope decisions (e.g., build vs. buy, MVP vs. full feature), they need financial context. Include budget constraints in the strategy document and domain descriptions. Example: "MVP budget: €0 (founder time only). First revenue target: €X by Q2 2027."

### Share Costs and Gains

Ensure people have a direct relationship to the costs and gains of their work:

- Connect outcomes to the people who created them
- Incentivize effectiveness and waste reduction
- Make the link between effort and impact visible

**For AI agent organizations:** Track the cost of agent operations (API tokens, compute time) and connect it to value delivered. When agents propose work, they should be aware of the effort/value tradeoff. Example: the Coordinator should flag if a polish sprint costs more in agent time than the pilot festival is worth.

### Open Salary

Create a fair, transparent compensation formula:

- Determine compensation by formula, not individual negotiation
- Make the formula and individual compensation transparent
- Consider factors: subsistence, needs, investment, productivity, merit
- Develop a transition strategy for existing compensation

**For AI agent organizations:** When the organization hires humans (contractors, employees), use an open salary formula. Agents can help draft and maintain the formula. This applies to the founders' equity split (already 50/50), future team member compensation, and contractor rates.

### Support Role

Apply the S3 role pattern to external contractors:

- Clarify the driver for the role
- Create a domain description (Delegation Canvas)
- Implement a selection process
- Limit the contract term with review points
- Build in regular peer reviews
- The contractor consents to take on the role

**For AI agent organizations:** When external humans (designers, developers, consultants) join the team, onboard them with the same role description pattern used for agents. They get a Delegation Canvas, boundaries, deliverables, and evaluation schedule. They participate in governance within their domain.

### Bylaws

Secure S3 principles in the organization's legal structure:

- Embed consent decision-making in legal bylaws
- Protect role selection processes
- Define organizational structure legally
- Secure values, owner influence, and cost/gain sharing
- Protect the organizational culture from legal erosion

**For AI agent organizations:** When the organization incorporates (GmbH, UG, etc.), ensure the legal structure supports S3 governance. The partnership agreement between Alex and Kirill should reflect consent decision-making for strategic decisions (as defined in Policy 002).

## Cultural Patterns

### Artful Participation

An individual commitment to act in ways that enable effective collaboration:

- Take responsibility for your own learning and development
- Consider the needs of the team alongside your own
- Refrain from or change actions that are not helpful
- Raise objections and concerns when you see them
- Support others in their participation

**For AI agent organizations:** Covered in collaboration.md. Every agent and human should practice artful participation — actively contributing to effectiveness, not just executing instructions.

### Involve Those Affected

When making decisions that impact others, involve them:

- Identify who is affected by the decision
- Include them (or their representative) in the decision process
- Include them when reviewing and evolving the decision later

**For AI agent organizations:** Covered in collaboration.md. Before making decisions that affect multiple agents, the Coordinator ensures affected agents are consulted.

### Invest in Ongoing Learning

Make continuous learning part of daily work:

- Embed learning into operations and role expectations
- After each sprint, identify what was learned
- Encourage experimentation and reflection
- Support people in developing competencies

**For AI agent organizations:** Covered in collaboration.md. Agents document lessons learned in PR descriptions; the Coordinator aggregates them in retrospectives.

### Breaking Agreements

When following a rule would cause harm, you can break it — with responsibility:

- Be certain the benefit outweighs the cost of waiting
- Clean up any disturbances
- Follow up with anyone affected
- Change the agreement properly to prevent repeated breaking

**For AI agent organizations:** Covered in collaboration.md. Agents flag the tension and escalate rather than breaking agreements unilaterally.

### Contract for Successful Collaboration

Co-create working agreements that set collaboration up for success:

- Define expectations and culture transparently
- Agree on terms before making them legally robust
- Use clear language with glossaries for technical terms
- Build in lifecycle support: onboarding, reviews, disagreement resolution, ending

**For AI agent organizations:** Covered in collaboration.md. The agent definition IS the contract — ensure it covers the full lifecycle.

## Bringing in S3

### Create a Pull System for Organizational Change

Create an environment that invites and enables change from within:

- Change things only when there is value in doing so
- Don't break what already works
- Meet people where they are — support manageable pace
- Distribute responsibility for culture development to everybody
- Invite people to run experiments when they discover requirements

**For AI agent organizations:** Don't impose all S3 patterns at once. Start with the foundation (driver, requirement, domains, roles) and add patterns as the organization encounters the problems they solve. Example: add the Retrospective pattern only after the first sprint reveals process issues.

### Be the Change

Lead by example — behave in the ways you want others to behave:

- Model the collaborative culture you want to build
- Practice consent, transparency, and empiricism yourself
- Others will follow when they see it working

**For AI agent organizations:** The founders model S3 behavior. When Alex reviews a PR, he practices consent (checking for objections, not just approving). When Kirill sends outreach, he's transparent about the experiment framing. Agents mirror the culture they're embedded in.

### Invite Change

Clarify the reason for change and invite participation:

- Be transparent about why the change is needed
- Clarify expectations and constraints
- Avoid coercion — let people choose
- Acknowledge skepticism
- Include affected people in evaluating outcomes

**For AI agent organizations:** When introducing new patterns or changing agent definitions, explain the driver (what problem are we solving?) and invite input. Example: "We're adding a Retrospective pattern because sprint 4 had 24 merge conflicts. Here's the proposed process — any objections?"
